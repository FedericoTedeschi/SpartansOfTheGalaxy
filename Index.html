About our project

Hi! We are the Spartans of the Galaxy and this is our project, addressing the Virtual Planetary Exploration 2.0 Challenge. In the next decade, humanity will go back to the Moon, to stay. However, sustainable missions to the Moon require the exploitation of local resources: while reusable rockets have lowered the cost of sending payload to space, extracting water and construction materials from lunar regolith will be fundamental for a quicker and cheaper development of a moonbase. Moreover, the Moon's soil is rich with rare metals useble in electronics, and with Helium-3, an isotope of helium that could fuel future nuclear fusion power plants.

For these reasons we want to educate the public about some of the ways we could localize, mine and transport lunar resources.

Excavator

A lunar excavator would be quite different from one on Earth. Thanks to the Moon's gravity being around one-sixth of Earth's gravity, it could lift more easily heavier loads. However, it needs adequate protection from the abrasive lunar dust. For safety reasons, it is preferred to have the excavator to be automated or teleoperated by astronauts or by Earth's ground stations. The complexity of automating such machines would surely require remote control for the first missions. NASA is currently developing a Lightweight Bulldozer Attachment for Construction and Excavation on the Lunar Surface (LANCE) blade, with the aim of building a resilient yet low weight blade.

Hauler

Once the lunar regolith is gathered, it will need an hauler to be transported from the mining site to the processing site. NASA is currently working on a six-legged rover, called All-Terrain Hex-Limbed Extra-Terrestrial Explorer (ATHLETE). This rover is expected to be remotely operated by astronauts on the Moon or from Earth. It can carry payloads up to 350 kg and is intended to be used as a delivery system for the Moonbase. While its wheels allow it to travel on stable soil, the 6 Degrees-of-Freedom limbs allow it to easily overcome terrain that is too soft, obstacles and steep terrain.

More about the ATHLETE rover here.

Drone

The model is a representation of the martian helicopter Ingenuity and such technology could not fly on the Moon. However it is still representative of what a drone would do on the moon.

The success of Ingenuity's mission paves the way for a whole new type of technologies for lunar exploration. The use of a flying drone, which due to the lack of atmosphere on the Moon would require a different type of propulsion than the classical blades we're used to, would open a wide range of possibilities. For example, a drone could map the surrounding areas and recognize areas of interest by localizing resources to be mined.
